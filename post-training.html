Foundations

What post-training is, and why it matters.
$ man post-training

Pre-training gives a language model its raw capability — predicting the next token across trillions of words. But a pre-trained model is essentially an autocomplete engine. It can't follow instructions, refuse harmful requests, or reason through multi-step problems. Post-training is the set of techniques that turns that autocomplete engine into something you can actually ship.

Modern post-training follows three stages, each solving a distinct problem. Skip any of them and the failure mode is immediate: skip SFT and the model can't follow instructions; skip preference tuning and it's capable but abrasive; skip RL and you don't get the emergent reasoning seen in models like DeepSeek-R1.

01
Supervised Fine-Tuning
Teach the model to follow instructions via curated input/output pairs. Next-token prediction on demonstrations of desired behavior.
02
Preference Tuning
Shape how the model responds. DPO, KTO, ORPO — the model learns from ranked pairs instead of absolute labels.
03
Reinforcement Learning
RLHF, GRPO, RLVR. Optimize against reward signals — human preferences, reward models, or verifiers — to elicit reasoning and tool use.

Each stage builds on the previous one, but the boundaries are blurring. ORPO merges SFT and preference tuning into one objective. RLVR replaces human preferences with automated verifiers. The trajectory is clear: every generation of methods removes a dependency.

[01]

Gradient Descent

The engine behind all learning.
$ trace grad_descent

Every technique in this guide — SFT, LoRA, DPO, GRPO — reduces to the same idea: define a scalar loss, compute its gradient with respect to the model's parameters, and take a small step in the opposite direction. Repeat until the loss stops dropping.

The loss quantifies how wrong the model is. For language modeling, it's the negative log-likelihood of the next token — a single scalar summarizing billions of parameters' worth of being-wrong.

The learning rate controls step size. Too large and updates overshoot good minima; too small and training stalls. Post-training typically uses — two to three orders of magnitude smaller than pre-training, because we're nudging an already-capable model rather than shaping raw behavior.

> backpropagation

How do you compute for a network with billions of parameters? You don't compute it directly. You apply the chain rule layer by layer, starting from the loss and walking backwards:

Each layer's gradient is a product of the gradient flowing in from above and the local derivative of its own output. Modern frameworks (PyTorch, JAX) build this computation graph automatically during the forward pass — you write the math, autograd writes the gradients.

Supervised Fine-Tuning

Teaching the model by example.
$ train --method sft

SFT takes a pre-trained model and trains it on curated (instruction, response) pairs. The objective is identical to pre-training — next-token prediction — but on data that demonstrates the behavior you want: helpful, correctly formatted, refusing when it should, reasoning step-by-step when asked.

Same cross-entropy objective as pre-training, masked to only score the response tokens. The model is penalized for putting low probability on the tokens an expert annotator wrote.

data quality > data quantity

The LIMA paper (2023) showed 1,000 carefully curated examples can produce surprisingly capable instruction-following. On the other end, Nemotron 3 Super used 7M samples aggressively filtered from a broader 40M-sample corpus. Both extremes share a principle: every sample is vetted.

Parameter-Efficient Methods

LoRA, QLoRA, and adapters — fine-tuning without breaking the bank.
$ train --method lora --rank 16

Full fine-tuning updates every weight. For a 70B model that's ~140GB of gradients and optimizer state on top of the weights themselves — out of reach for most hardware. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) freezes the base model and trains a small delta.

RLHF

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback — the technique that gave us ChatGPT.
$ train --method rlhf --algo ppo

SFT teaches the model what good answers look like. RLHF teaches it which of several plausible answers humans actually prefer. The pipeline has four moving parts:

Start with a supervised fine-tuned model that already follows instructions. RLHF refines how it responds — it doesn't teach it to follow instructions from scratch.
Sample pairs of outputs from the SFT model. Humans read both responses and mark the better one. You need tens of thousands of such comparisons. This is the rate-limiter of the whole pipeline — and the motivation for RLAIF.
Train a neural network to predict human preferences from the labeled pairs. The reward model is typically initialized from the SFT model with a scalar head. The loss is a Bradley-Terry logistic regression on score differences.
The policy model generates responses, the reward model scores them, and PPO updates the policy to maximize reward — with a KL penalty pulling it back toward the reference (SFT) model so it doesn't drift into gibberish that happens to hack the reward.

> PPO with KL constraint

The KL term is what keeps RLHF from collapsing. Without it, the policy quickly discovers degenerate outputs that maximize the reward model but are nonsensical — textbook reward hacking.

four models in memory

RLHF requires simultaneous copies of the policy, reference, reward, and critic models during training. For a 70B policy that's memory-painful before you've done a single optimizer step. This overhead is the original sin that DPO, GRPO, and friends were designed to eliminate.

> RLAIF & Constitutional AI

Human preference labels are the bottleneck. RLAIF (Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback) replaces the human annotator with a larger or differently-prompted LLM. Anthropic's Constitutional AI goes further: a set of written principles guides an AI critic to revise harmful outputs, and the resulting preferences train the policy. The practical upshot is unlimited preference data, compute-bound rather than label-bound.

DPO & Variants

Skipping the reward model entirely — direct alignment from preferences.
$ train --method dpo

Rafailov et al. (2023) proved something beautiful: under the KL-constrained RLHF objective, the optimal policy has a closed-form solution. The policy is the reward function, up to a normalization constant. You don't need to train a separate reward model and run RL on it — you can optimize the policy directly from preference pairs.

> SimPO — drop the reference model

Meng et al. (2024) noticed that the reference model in DPO is just providing length-normalization and a regularization anchor. Replace both with an explicit length-normalized objective and a margin:

One model in memory instead of two. In practice SimPO matches or beats DPO on most benchmarks.

> KTO — loss aversion as an objective

Ethayarajh et al. (2024) drew on Kahneman-Tversky prospect theory: losing $100 feels worse than gaining $100 feels good. KTO bakes this asymmetry directly into the loss function — the model gets punished harder for undesirable outputs than it gets rewarded for good ones. The practical win is that KTO works on unpaired data: you only need a binary "good / bad" label per sample, not a ranked pair.

> ORPO — SFT and alignment in one pass

Hong et al. (2024) went the other direction: instead of removing stages, merge them. ORPO adds a contrastive odds-ratio term to the standard SFT loss. The SFT term teaches format; the odds-ratio term teaches preference. One stage, no reward model, no reference model:

GRPO & DAPO

The algorithms behind DeepSeek-R1 — eliminating the critic model entirely.
$ train --method grpo --group-size 8

PPO needs a critic model to estimate the value function . Training a good critic is hard — and for a 70B policy, the critic is another 70B of GPU. GRPO (DeepSeek, 2024) makes a simpler observation: you don't need an absolute value estimate to compute useful advantages. You just need relative scores within a group of samples.

For each prompt, sample responses, score them with a reward model (or verifier), and normalize within the group. Above-average responses get positive advantages; below-average get negative. The critic is replaced by arithmetic.

> the GRPO objective

Same clipped surrogate as PPO, but with group-relative advantages and no critic. DeepSeek-R1 used GRPO on verifier-based rewards to produce chain-of-thought reasoning that rivals frontier proprietary models.

> DAPO — stabilizing long-horizon RL

DAPO (ByteDance/Tsinghua, 2025) isolates the instabilities that hit GRPO when chain-of-thought outputs get long. It introduces four patches — token-level policy gradient loss (per-token normalization instead of per-sequence), clip-higher (asymmetric clipping that lets low-probability tokens update more freely), dynamic sampling (drop prompts where every sample in the group gets the same reward — zero gradient signal), and overlong-reward shaping (penalize runaway generations). On AIME 2024, DAPO reached 50 points using Qwen2.5-32B as the base — the first open-source RL system to hit frontier math-reasoning scores.

Reasoning & RLVR

Verifiable rewards, self-play, and the emergence of reasoning capabilities.
$ train --method grpo --reward verify

The expensive part of RLHF is the reward model — training it requires human labels, and the final quality is capped by human judgment. For math, code, and structured reasoning, you don't need that. A unit test passes or fails. A proof checker accepts or rejects. A math expression is correct or isn't.

RLVR — Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards — swaps the reward model for a verifier. Faster, cheaper, more consistent. The reward signal is ground truth rather than a learned approximation.

emergent reasoning behaviors

When trained with RLVR and enough compute, models start exhibiting behaviors nobody wrote a prompt for: self-verification, backtracking, working through multiple approaches, catching their own arithmetic mistakes mid-derivation. DeepSeek-R1's training trace showed extended chains of thought emerging spontaneously as the policy discovered they produced higher verified-reward rates.

> self-play and closed loops

Combine RLVR with a model that can generate its own training data and you get a closed loop: the model identifies its weaknesses by where it fails verification, synthesizes harder problems in those regions, trains on them, and repeats. No human in the loop. The limits of this loop are an active research question — and arguably the most important one in the field.

> agentic RL

The newest frontier is training models for multi-step tool use. NVIDIA's NeMo Gym exposes interactive RL environments supporting multi-turn rollouts, tool-calling verification, and decoupled agent/environment architectures. Nemotron 3 Super was trained across 21 environment configurations producing 1.2 million rollouts. This is the template for how models become agents: RL on tasks where the reward only resolves after a sequence of tool calls.

Advanced Topics

Model merging, distillation, continual learning — the broader toolkit.
$ ls /opt/post-training/advanced

> model merging

Take two fine-tuned checkpoints from the same base model and average their weights. The result frequently inherits capabilities from both parents:

More sophisticated variants (TIES-merging, DARE, Task Arithmetic) resolve sign conflicts and prune redundant parameters before averaging. Merging works best when models share a common base and architecture. Many of the top open-source models on public leaderboards are merges of specialist fine-tunes, not single monolithic runs.

> knowledge distillation

Train a small student model to match a large teacher's output distribution. The student learns from soft targets (logits over the full vocabulary) instead of hard labels:

The temperature softens both distributions, surfacing the teacher's uncertainty about nearby tokens — a richer signal than the argmax alone. This is how most small frontier-adjacent models get their surprising capability-per-parameter.

> continual learning

Fine-tune a model on task B after task A and it will often forget A — catastrophic forgetting. Elastic Weight Consolidation (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017) penalizes movement in parameters that were important for task A, letting less-critical weights absorb task B. For production pipelines with repeated domain updates, some form of continual-learning constraint is the difference between a model that stays useful and one that regresses on every refresh.

> common pitfalls

  • reward hacking — policy finds degenerate outputs that score high but aren't what you want. Keep on the KL term. Hold out a diverse eval set.
  • distribution shift — SFT data and preference-tuning data come from different distributions. ORPO dodges this by unifying stages; otherwise keep the SFT distribution represented in downstream data.
  • overfitting to the reward model — the RM is a proxy, not the objective. Its calibration drifts as the policy does. Refresh preferences periodically.
  • catastrophic forgetting — a fresh post-training run can silently regress on benchmarks you didn't re-measure. Maintain a frozen regression suite.
  • under-powered group size in GRPO — too-small means noisy advantage estimates. Start at 8, go to 16 on harder tasks.

> the arc of the field

Post-training has evolved from a single technique (RLHF) into a modular ecosystem. The trend is unmistakable: each generation of methods removes a dependency. RLHF needed four models and human annotators. DPO removed the reward model and critic. SimPO removed the reference model. ORPO merged SFT and alignment into one stage. RLVR replaced human labels with automated verification. We're converging on systems that improve themselves — not through magic, but through the same math of gradient descent applied to progressively better objectives.
